About

I work where letters misbehave—where Copperplate and Blackletter jump the fence and run with the street. My practice blends calligraphy, calligraffiti, and gestural abstraction to test a simple question: what happens when language stops explaining and starts bearing weight? The stroke becomes a heartbeat; a word becomes scaffolding; a line becomes a place to stand.

Materials matter. I paint on whatever the world gives me—cardboard, gessobord, canvas, paper—because surfaces carry stories. Cardboard is honest; it keeps no secrets about the lives it has touched. The grid—Fibonacci boxes, baselines, and margins—arrives courtesy of my data-analyst brain; the breakages and drips come from the rest of me.

I’m Southern by formation and forward-looking by necessity. Magnolia motifs, liturgical fragments (“Sursum Corda,” “Resurgam”), and street idioms (“Have Hope,” “In Love”) recur as anchors. The work explores resilience under pressure, chosen family, public dignity, memory and silence—and the stubborn little light that refuses to go out.

What the Work Explores

  • Language as structure: letters begin as writing and end as architecture—rooms for breath, rooms for standing taller.
  • Public dignity: text in public space, the hand as witness, and the moral weather of the street.
  • Memory & the South: magnolia, hymn, and vernacular as living archives—Southern Gothic without the costume.
  • Hope: not the Hallmark kind—the muscular, stubborn kind that survives field tests.

Materials & Methods

  • Acrylics & inks on cardboard, gessobord, canvas, and paper.
  • Grids (Fibonacci, baselines) meet drips, scumbles, and glaze layers.
  • Tools: dip pens, brush pens, Posca, Tombow, sable and synthetic rounds/flats.
  • Palette tendencies: burnt orange notes, Prussian blues, iridescent whites, deep blacks.
